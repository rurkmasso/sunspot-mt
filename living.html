Things only locals know

Read this if you're new to Malta.

01

The wind decides the day

Majjistral (NW wind):south-east is calm — try St Peter's Pool, Marsascala, Pretty Bay.
Grigal (NE wind):west is calm — Għajn Tuffieħa, Ġnejna, Fomm ir-Riħ.
Xlokk (SE wind):bad everywhere on the south, north is OK.
Check Windguru Maltabefore you leave.

02

Weekday is the new weekend

Weekends are tourist crush. The same beach on a Tuesday morningis half empty. If your job allows it, swap your Saturday for a Tuesday lunch break — life-changing.

03

Avoid feast-day weekends

Every village has a festain June–September. Local beaches near a festa get rammed (the band club bus brings everyone). Check VisitMalta's festa calendar— go to a different region that weekend.

04

The 9am rule

For any sandy beach in summer: if you arrive after 9am on a weekend, the front rows are gone and you're sweating up the car park. Pre-book a sunbed on Sunspot the night before. Sleep in. Show up at 11.

05

Sea jellies (lampuki season)

Late August / September brings mauve stingerson north shores. Sting hurts but isn't dangerous. Avoid Mellieha when the red flag is up; head to Comino or Marsascala which usually escape it.

06

Septembrers eat best

Septemberis the locals' month — sea is warmest (26°C), tourist crowds gone, lampuki in season, prices drop 20%. If you can take leave any month, take it then.

07

Free buses on the islands

Tallinja is €2 (€1.50 off-peak). Buy the Tallinja Cardat any post office for €15 + €0.75/ride. Cheaper if you commute. Routes to most beaches exist; check publictransport.com.mt.

08

Sliema parking is psychological warfare

Don't drive to Sliema between 18:00 Friday and 22:00 Sunday in summer. Take a bus, a Bolt, or your bike. The half hour saved circling is a real quality-of-life gain.

For expats moving here

Language? English everywhere.

Maltese is the national language but English is co-official. Every sign, every menu, every staff member speaks English. Italian is widely understood.

Learning Maltese is welcome but not necessary. Most expats pick up bonġu, grazzi, melaand call it a day.

Beach safety

Lifeguarded beaches in summer: Mellieha, Golden Bay, Għajn Tuffieħa, Pretty Bay, Ramla l-Ħamra (Gozo).

Other beaches: swim at your own risk. Red flag= don't swim. Yellow flag= caution.

European Health Insurance Card (EHIC / GHIC) covers visits to Mater Dei hospital.

Pharmacy chain

Coastal pharmacies don't run out: Lifeline, Brown's, Chemimarthave beach essentials (reef-safe SPF, jellyfish vinegar, aloe). 24h rotating duty pharmacy — check the door of any pharmacy for the duty roster.

Bike-to-beach

Cycle network is limited but the Sliema → St Julian's → Pembroke promenadeis fully cyclable. Use BOV Tallinja Bikestations (€1/30min).

Don't try to cycle to Mellieha — the coast road has no shoulder and Maltese drivers are not bicycle-aware.
